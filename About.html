Nature's Serenity
Let's explore the diverse and enchanting natural spots of Kerala.
Munnar
Munnar, located in the Idukki district, is a hill station famed for its vast tea gardens, picturesque valleys, and cool climate. It stands around 1,600 meters above sea level and has been a popular destination since the British colonial era. The landscape is marked by misty mountains, layered tea plantations, and dramatic viewpoints like Echo Point and Top Station. Nature enthusiasts can also explore the Eravikulam National Park, home to the endangered Nilgiri Tahr and vibrant species of flora, including the rare Neelakurinji flower, which blooms once every twelve years.
Alleppey
Alleppey is renowned for its unique network of backwaters—serene stretches of lakes, canals, and rivers that offer a distinct sense of tranquility. A houseboat cruise here is an iconic experience, allowing travelers to drift along the waters while witnessing traditional village life, paddy fields, and coconut groves. The annual Nehru Trophy Snake Boat Race, held in August, draws visitors from around the world. Sunset at Marari Beach and the historic Alleppey Lighthouse are also highlights for anyone visiting Alleppey.
Wayanad
Nestled in the Western Ghats, Wayanad is a blend of scenic landscapes, dense forests, and abundant wildlife. The Edakkal Caves here contain ancient petroglyphs that date back thousands of years, revealing the area’s rich prehistoric significance. Chembra Peak, the highest point in Wayanad, offers a popular trek and features a heart-shaped lake halfway up, creating a memorable experience for adventure seekers. Other notable spots include the Wayanad Wildlife Sanctuary, Banasura Sagar Dam, and Soochipara Waterfalls, all of which showcase the area's rich biodiversity and natural beauty.
Thekkady
Thekkady, home to the Periyar Wildlife Sanctuary, is one of India’s largest wildlife reserves. The Periyar Lake, situated within the sanctuary, is famous for boat rides where visitors may catch glimpses of elephants, sambar deer, and other wildlife in their natural environment. The sanctuary’s dense evergreen forests and semi-evergreen vegetation provide a habitat for Bengal tigers, leopards, and a variety of bird species. The spice plantations of Thekkady are another highlight, allowing visitors to experience the aromas of cardamom, pepper, and vanilla.
Low Land
Kuttanad
the "Rice Bowl of Kerala," Kuttanad is a scenic lowland area covering parts of Alappuzha, Kottayam, and Pathanamthitta districts. Known for its extensive paddy fields and traditional farming below sea level, it's a prime location for backwater tourism.
Alappuzha
Known for its network of canals, lagoons, and picturesque backwaters, Alappuzha (Alleppey) is popular for houseboat cruises and lush scenery. It's a flat coastal area where visitors can experience village life, coir industries, and serene water routes.
Vembanad Lake
Spanning multiple districts, this is the longest lake in India and a vital part of Kerala's lowland ecosystem. It provides opportunities for fishing, boating, and witnessing traditional coir processing along its banks.
Chavara
Spanning multiple districts, this is the longest lake in India and a vital part of Kerala's lowland ecosystem. It provides opportunities for fishing, boating, and witnessing traditional coir processing along its banks.
Mid Land
Wayanad
Known for its lush green landscapes, hills, and spice plantations, Wayanad is a prime midland area. It’s rich in biodiversity, with places like the Wayanad Wildlife Sanctuary, which shelters elephants, leopards, and unique flora.
Thirssur
The cultural capital of Kerala, Thrissur is known for its annual Pooram festival. It features the iconic Vadakkunnathan Temple and midland landscapes with fertile lands ideal for coconut and paddy cultivation.
Malayattoor
Located near the Periyar River, these areas are popular for their scenic beauty, pilgrimage sites, and the Kodanad Elephant Training Centre. The rolling hills, dense forests, and rivers create a classic midland ambiance.
Idukki
Though partly highland, much of Idukki’s area falls in the midland region, known for its tea plantations, rolling hills, and the Idukki Dam. Places like Vagamon and Thodupuzha offer a blend of midland and highland landscapes with cool climates and rich biodiversity.
High Land
Munnar
Famous for its sprawling tea plantations and misty mountains, Munnar in the Western Ghats is one of Kerala's top tourist destinations. Its high elevation provides a cool climate, ideal for tea, coffee, and spice cultivation. The Eravikulam National Park here is home to the endangered Nilgiri Tahr.
Wayanad Highlands
Parts of Wayanad extend into highland territory, particularly around the Banasura Sagar Dam and Chembra Peak, which is one of Kerala's highest points. These areas are known for trekking, waterfalls, and breathtaking views of the Western Ghats.
Thekkady
Located near the Periyar Wildlife Sanctuary, Thekkady is known for its wildlife, spice plantations, and rolling hills. It’s a biodiversity hotspot where you can spot elephants, tigers, and various bird species, making it a prime highland area for nature enthusiasts.
Agasthyakoodam
This peak in the Western Ghats is both a pilgrimage site and a biodiversity hotspot, known for its medicinal plants. It’s accessible only through trekking and is home to a variety of unique flora and fauna, making it one of Kerala's most ecologically rich highland regions.
Gods Own Country
Food
Kerala cuisine is a vibrant tapestry of flavors and spices, celebrated for iconic dishes like appam with rich, creamy stew, the layered Kerala parotta with spicy beef or chicken curry, and the hearty puttu paired with kadala curry. Sadya, a grand vegetarian feast served on banana leaves, is a must-try, offering a variety of curries, pickles, and payasam for dessert. The coastal flavors shine in dishes like fish moilee and meen pollichathu, while the aromatic Malabar biryani brings a touch of North Kerala’s unique taste, making Kerala a true paradise for food lovers.
Culture
Kerala's culture is a beautiful fusion of traditions, festivals, and art forms that reflect its rich heritage. Known as "God's Own Country," it celebrates unique classical dances like Kathakali with its elaborate costumes and storytelling, and Mohiniyattam, the graceful "dance of the enchantress." Festivals like Onam, with its iconic flower carpets (pookalam) and traditional feasts, and Vishu, marked by vibrant rituals and celebrations, highlight Kerala’s spirit of unity. The state is also home to ancient martial art Kalaripayattu, colorful temple festivals with elephant processions, and a love for literature and Ayurveda, making Kerala's culture deeply diverse and spiritually enriching.
Nature
Kerala's nature is a breathtaking blend of lush greenery, serene backwaters, and mist-covered hills, earning it the title "God’s Own Country." The state boasts pristine beaches like Varkala and Kovalam, scenic hill stations such as Munnar and Wayanad with sprawling tea plantations, and tranquil backwaters in Alleppey and Kumarakom where houseboats glide gently. Dense forests and wildlife sanctuaries like Periyar and Silent Valley shelter a diverse range of flora and fauna, while rivers, waterfalls like Athirappilly, and rich biodiversity make Kerala’s landscape a paradise for nature lovers and a testament to its natural beauty.
Festival
Kerala's festivals are vibrant celebrations that reflect the state’s rich cultural heritage and unity. Onam, the grand harvest festival, is celebrated with elaborate feasts, colorful pookalams (floral decorations), Vallamkali (boat races), and traditional dance performances. Vishu marks the Malayali New Year with the ritual of Vishukkani, symbolizing prosperity and good fortune. Thrissur Pooram, one of the most spectacular temple festivals, showcases magnificent elephant processions, traditional drumming, and fireworks. Additionally, festivals like Eid, Christmas, and Diwali are celebrated with equal enthusiasm, creating a harmonious blend of diverse traditions that adds to Kerala's cultural richness.
